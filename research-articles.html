Machine learning for Internet of Things data analysis: A survey

Article Citation: Mahdavinejad, Mohammad Saeid, et al. "Machine learning for Internet of Things data analysis: A survey." Digital Communications and Networks 4.3 (2018): 161-175.

The goals of this survey article are to discuss the evolution of data hand in hand with machine learning techniques. Further, it discusses how machine learning can be used to extract higher levels of information, essentially at it simplest terms: data. It discusses the potential techniques associated with such.

The general tasks that the methods address in this article is how machine learning can algorithmically be applied to higher levels of data information or smart data, the taxonomy of such algorithms, and why smart city is a typical use case in regards to the "Internet of Things".

The leading methods introduced and discussed within this paper follow the same structure of the general tasks that I have already previously discussed in the general tasks of this paper, but of course, follow along those tasks more in-depth. For example, when regarding the "Internet of Things" (IoT) architecture, applications of such heavily invoke Communication protocols, computing frameworks, the architecture itself, and smart city segments. The paper heavily finds its focus in the newness (relative sense because of the date that the paper was written, that being nearly five years ago) of the concept of IoT and the navigation of such with machine learning algorithms and data parsing.

In regards to IoT specifically, the context of the method discussions are heavily focused on "smart city" use cases and how the implementation of such in urban settings is a great example of why machine learning algorithms are essential in data access and analysis. In simpler terms, the digitization (heavily emphasized in a computational sense, not in the literal sense of the word) of urbanization is referenced as the primary demonstration of these applications of machine learning algorithms.

These methods could certainly be used in my own work, definitely with less of an emphasis on smart cities, but rather using the techniques of the machine learning algorithms to assist me in my data analysis. Essentially, making use of these algorithms in my own work both invoke things that I am certainly passionate about and that I feel that would highly benefit my work tremendously.

A Literature Review of Music in Computer Science

Article Citation: Warnars, Harco, and Winston Rusli. "A Literature Review of Music in Computer Science." International Journal of Computing and Digital System (2021).

The goal of this article is discussing the relations of computer science and music and how a close relationship between the two situationally can be incredibly beneficial to both art forms, despite the computer science not traditionally being viewed as an art form by outside perspectives.

The main goal of this article is essentially the combination or collaboration between computer science and music. This main goal is split into sections, starting with discussions of the rise in popularity and general notoriety of digital music, the integration of computer science into music education, and further into the concept of music creation using computer science.

These goals may seem quite different when viewed individually but throughout the article they tie together rather well, discussing already existing applications for each of the individual sections and ultimately creating the relationship between the existing three and tying it to the evolution of the music industry through digitization. It considers the potential negative impacts of such but tries to bring attention to the positives.

Data portability effects on data-driven innovation of online platforms: Analyzing Spotify

Article Citation: Ramos, Esmeralda Florez, and Knut Blind. "Data portability effects on data-driven innovation of online platforms: Analyzing Spotify." Telecommunications Policy 44.9 (2020): 102026.

The goal of this article is to discuss the aspects of data collection and portability, specifically with Spotify and views such within the scope of the European Union (EU), specifically within the EU General Data Protection Regulation. Additionally, it discusses the competitors within the scope of data portability, including Spotify, Google, and Facebook.

The main goal of this article is to inform the potential effects (truthfully, we could observe such in real time because this article is from three years ago) of data portability in online applications, specifically in the bigger competitors in the context of online platforms.

More specifically, the article discusses the effects of data portability in regards to Spotify and heavily emphasizes the potential implications of such in the music platform's evolution and ultimately its success. It explores all aspects of how Spotify utilizes data and even references data from Spotify specifically to cover all of their figurative bases in the potential implications and effects this policy within the EU.

As an unofficial note, I understand evaluating data portability with Spotify in the scope of the EU may not seem incredibly relevant, however, the inclusion of such in my research is incredibly helpful in understanding the impact in my work and also helps foster thoughts involving the ethicality in my work and ensuring I am being ethical in my technical work, specifically with data collection. Fortunately, Spotify has already kept me checked and balanced in the sense that collecting and storing user data (within databases especially) is a violation of their terms of service, thus there was no room for error in the sense of ethicality.
